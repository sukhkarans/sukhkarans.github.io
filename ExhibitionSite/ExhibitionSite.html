There are currently three levels of EV chargers.

  • Level 1 chargers use standard household 120 volt AC electricity to charge, thus require no extra equipment to use and can plug directly into a wall outlet. Level 1 charging is the slowest of the three levels and provides about 2 to 5 miles of range per 1 hour of charging.
  • Level 2 chargers use the same type of connection as Level 1 chargers (J1772 connectors), but are powered by 208/240 volt AC, so these chargers require the installation of home charging equipment. Most EV owners will opt to install Level 2 chargers at their homes to charge their vehicles overnight, and these chargers provide 10 to 20 miles of range per 1 hour of charging.
  • Level 3 chargers, also referred to as DC Fast Chargers because they use direct current (DC) as opposed to alternating current (AC) as used by the first two levels, use 480 volt electricity and come in three different connector types: CCS, CHAdeMO, and Tesla. Level 3 chargers provide 60 to 80 miles of range per 20 minutes of charging. Level 3 chargers are not suitable for home installation.

It is important to note that not every EV supports all three levels of charging, and even then, currently no EVs can support the full amount of power Level 3 chargers can output. And of course, not all EVs use the same type of connector, but currently the CHAdeMO connector is the most popular for non-Tesla EVs.

EVs still lack the large scale infrastructure and compatability needed for mass adoption. Charging stations are not nearly as close by as gas stations are, not all of stations are made to the same standards, and they're not required to support every type of connector. This can cause large variations in vehicle recharge times as well as limit where you can go, both to recharge or to just travel.

Source: Forbes

A study by AAA found that the effective range for an EV is dramatically reduced in extreme temperatures when using heating or air conditioning. On average, when driving at 20 degrees with the heat off, the range drops by 12%, but with the heat on, it falls to 41% of the usual range. Similarly, but not as severe a drop, in 95 degree weather, battery range can fall by 4% with the AC off, and 17% with AC on.
